Who needs an eTA to visit Canada

An electronic travel authorization (eTA) is required for most visa-exempt travellers flying to or through Canada. Find out if you need one before you book your trip.

Who needs an eTA

You need an eTA if you are a visa-exempt foreign national and you are flying to or through Canada.

Visa-exempt travellers

Citizens of visa-exempt countries must have an eTA to board a flight to Canada — including connecting flights through Canadian airports.

Check the list of visa-exempt countries.

Citizens of select visa-required countries

Some citizens of visa-required countries may apply for an eTA instead of a visitor visa when flying to Canada.

Check the list of eligible visa-required countries.

Arriving by land or sea

If you arrive by car, bus, train, or boat (including cruise ships), you do not need an eTA — even if you are visa-exempt. A valid passport or accepted travel document is still required.

Who is exempt

The following travellers do not need an eTA:

Canadian citizens

Canadian citizens — including dual citizens — do not need an eTA. Dual citizens must travel with a valid Canadian passport.

American-Canadians may use a valid Canadian or U.S. passport.

Canadian permanent residents

Permanent residents do not need an eTA. You must carry a valid permanent resident (PR) card or PR travel document.

U.S. citizens

U.S. citizens are exempt from the eTA. You must carry proper identification, such as a valid U.S. passport.

Lawful permanent residents of the U.S.

You must show both of the following for all methods of travel to Canada:

  • a valid passport from your country of nationality (or an equivalent travel document)
  • a valid green card or equivalent proof of U.S. status
Complete list of acceptable U.S. status documents
  • valid permanent resident card (Form I-551)
  • foreign passport with an unexpired temporary I-551 stamp (ADIT stamp)
  • foreign passport with a temporary I-551 printed notation on a machine-readable immigrant visa with a U.S. Customs and Border Protection admission stamp
  • expired Form I-551 with Form I-797 for pending Form I-751 or Form I-829
  • expired Form I-551 with Form I-797 for pending Form I-90
  • valid re-entry permit (Form I-327)
  • Form I-94 with an unexpired temporary I-551 stamp (ADIT stamp) and a passport-style photo

Visa-required travellers

If you are from a visa-required country (including alien's passport holders and stateless individuals), you need a visitor visa — not an eTA.

Former permanent residents

PR status does not expire. If you once lived in Canada — even many years ago — you may still have PR status. Learn how to avoid travel delays if you are not sure of your PR status.

See the complete list of eTA exemptions.

Entry requirements for all travellers

Even with a valid eTA, you must meet all of Canada's entry requirements. A border services officer will assess your eligibility when you arrive.

You must have

  • a valid travel document, such as a passport
  • a valid eTA or visitor visa
  • enough money for your stay

You must be able to show

  • you are in good health
  • you have no criminal or immigration-related convictions
  • you have ties to your home country — such as a job, home, financial assets, or family — that will bring you back
  • you will leave Canada at the end of your visit

Inadmissibility

An eTA does not guarantee entry to Canada

When you arrive, a border services officer will ask to see your passport and other documents. You must satisfy the officer that you are eligible to enter.

You may be found inadmissible for reasons including:

  • involvement in criminal activity
  • human rights violations
  • organized crime
  • security, health, or financial reasons

Find out more about inadmissibility.

Ready to apply?

Once you know you need an eTA, the application takes about 5 minutes and costs CAD $7.
