Types of work permits - Test

If you want to work in Canada temporarily, you need a work permit. The type of work permit you need depends on your job, your employer, and your situation.

On this page

  • Open work permits
  • Employer-specific work permits
  • Labour Market Impact Assessment (LMIA)
  • LMIA-exempt work permits
  • How to choose the right permit

Open work permits

An open work permit lets you work for almost any employer in Canada. You do not need a job offer to apply.

You may be eligible for an open work permit if you are:

  • the spouse or common-law partner of a skilled worker or international student
  • a recent graduate from a Canadian post-secondary institution (Post-Graduation Work Permit)
  • a refugee claimant or protected person
  • a participant in a working holiday program
  • a vulnerable worker in an exploitative situation

Post-Graduation Work Permit (PGWP)

If you graduated from an eligible Canadian school, you may be able to work anywhere in Canada for up to 3 years. The PGWP is one of the most common open work permits.

Employer-specific work permits

An employer-specific work permit — also called a closed work permit — ties you to one employer. Your permit will list:

  • the name of your employer
  • the location where you can work
  • how long you can work

You must have a valid job offer from a Canadian employer before you apply. If you want to change employers, you must apply for a new work permit.

Labour Market Impact Assessment (LMIA)

Many employer-specific work permits require your employer to get a Labour Market Impact Assessment (LMIA) from Employment and Social Development Canada (ESDC) before you apply.

An LMIA confirms that:

  • there is a need for a foreign worker to fill the job
  • no Canadian worker or permanent resident is available to do the job

High-wage positions

Jobs that pay at or above the provincial or territorial median wage. Employers must submit a transition plan showing how they will reduce their reliance on temporary foreign workers over time.

Low-wage positions

Jobs that pay below the provincial or territorial median wage. There are caps on the proportion of low-wage temporary foreign workers an employer can hire.

LMIA-exempt work permits

Some workers do not need an LMIA because of international agreements or Canadian interests. These include workers coming under:

International trade agreements (CUSMA, CETA, CPTPP)

Certain professionals, traders, investors, and intra-company transferees from the United States, Mexico, and other partner countries can work in Canada without an LMIA under free trade agreements.

Intra-company transfers

Multinational companies can temporarily transfer workers to a Canadian branch, subsidiary, or affiliate. The worker must have worked for the company for at least one year in a managerial, executive, or specialized knowledge role.

Significant benefit to Canada

Workers in unique positions — such as elite athletes, performing artists, or religious workers — may qualify on the basis that their work provides a significant cultural, social, or economic benefit to Canadians.

Reciprocal employment

Programs such as International Experience Canada (IEC) allow young people from partner countries to work in Canada, based on reciprocal opportunities for Canadians abroad.

How to choose the right permit

Use the table below to find the permit type that matches your situation.

Work permit types by situation
Your situation Permit type LMIA required
Graduated from a Canadian school Open (PGWP) No
Spouse of a skilled worker or student Open (spousal) No
Young person on a working holiday Open (IEC) No
Job offer — general position Employer-specific Usually yes
Job offer — trade agreement (e.g., CUSMA) Employer-specific No
Intra-company transfer Employer-specific No

Make sure you apply for the right permit

Applying for the wrong type of work permit can delay your application or result in a refusal. If you are unsure which permit applies to your situation, use the eligibility tool before you apply.
