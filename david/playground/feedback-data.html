The bottom line

The feedback widget has become IRCC's largest unanswered help desk.

Read a few hundred comments and a pattern is impossible to miss: people aren't critiquing pages, they're stuck mid-task and reaching for the nearest text box. The dominant failure isn't tone or typos — it's wayfinding. Visitors arrive with clear intent ("apply for an eTA," "check my status," "find the form") and can't see the door. Three findings carry the story.

Finding 01 · Findability

People can't find the form or the front door.

3,228

comments are some version of "where is the form?" or "where do I apply / what's the link?" — about 18% of everything. The site's core job, routing intent to the right application, is breaking at the moment of intent.

Finding 02 · Concentration

The pain is concentrated, not scattered.

60.5%

of all feedback lands on roughly a dozen pages — almost all of them transactional gateways: check status, find forms, sign in. Feedback here isn't curiosity; it's a blocked transaction.

Finding 03 · Wrong channel

The box is being used as search & support.

1 in 10

comments end in a question mark and 1,498 shout in ALL CAPS. People expect a reply. They're treating a one-way feedback field like a chatbot — and hearing silence.

How much, and when

A steady drumbeat — roughly 600 frustrated visitors a day

Volume never lets up. Weekdays run hot (Thursday is the worst day), weekends cool by a third, and the English-to-French ratio holds steady at about 2.5 : 1. The June 19 dip is just a partial collection day. There is no "quiet period" to wait for — this is the baseline.

Where it hurts

A handful of pages absorb most of the complaints

English and French versions merged to their true page; the ten largest are shown. Together with their smaller variants these page groups hold 60.5% of all feedback. The leaders are the transactional spine of IRCC's online service: checking status, finding forms, and signing in. When these pages generate feedback, a person is almost always blocked from finishing something they came to do.

What they're struggling with

The shape of the friction

Every comment was scanned for the needs and obstacles people describe (a comment can touch more than one). Visiting Canada dominates the topic mix, but the cross-cutting story underneath is logistics: finding a form, tracking an application, getting into an account. People mostly aren't asking whether they qualify — they're asking how and where.

Feedback over time

The friction doesn't fluctuate — it recurs

Tracking each theme's share of feedback across the four full weeks reveals the most important finding of all: the mix barely moves. eTA/visa sits near 18–20% every single week; form-finding hovers around 12%; status near 8%. These flat lines aren't boring — they're the diagnosis. This isn't a spike from a bad release or a seasonal blip you can wait out. The same problems resurface every week because they're baked into the page architecture. Until the pages change, the complaints won't.

The channel mismatch

People think they're talking to someone

The strongest behavioural signal in this dataset isn't a topic — it's a misunderstanding. The feedback box is one-way, but visitors use it as a search bar, a help line, and a place to plead their case. They write questions, they write in their own language, they write in capitals to be heard. Each of these is a design signal.

Why it matters: a meaningful slice of this volume could be deflected upstream. Search-style fragments suggest on-page search or a visible "Apply / Sign in / Check status" action would have ended the visit successfully. Non-Latin comments suggest the audience is more international than an EN/FR site assumes — and that machine-translation or clearer global signposting is worth testing.

When the friction happens

Peak frustration is mid-afternoon, Ottawa time

Feedback clusters between 14:00 and 19:00 UTC — late morning to mid-afternoon across Canadian time zones — overlapping business hours and call-centre demand. Device mix tells its own story: nearly a fifth of comments come from phones, where long forms and PDF downloads punish hardest.

What to do about it

Four moves, in priority order

The data points to fixes that are concrete and mostly cheap relative to their reach. Sequenced by impact-over-effort.

1Priority · Now

Put the action above the fold on the top pages

On Check status, Find forms and Sign in, make the primary action (Start / Sign in / Check status / Download the form) the most prominent, unmissable element. ~3,200 "where is it?" comments are a direct measurement of buttons people couldn't see.

2Priority · Now

Fix or front the status tracker

The status page is the single largest complaint source and comments cite outages. Confirm uptime, and when it's down show a plain-language status banner and an alternative — instead of letting 2,280 people hit a wall and reach for the feedback box.

3Priority · Next

Turn the feedback box into a deflection point

One in five comments is a search fragment and one in ten is a question. Add on-page search or surface "Top tasks" / suggested links right where the box lives, so a question becomes a click instead of a dead end.

4Priority · Test

Design for a global, mobile, multilingual audience

596 comments in other languages and 21.5% on mobile say the real audience is broader than EN/FR desktop. Test clearer international signposting, machine-translation hints, and mobile-first form/PDF flows.
